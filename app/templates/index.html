Information

This NSF funded research program focuses on technological innovations with potential for significant economic and societal impact. A pressing social and environmental issue on a national scale is the effect of stormwater on waterbody impairment, particularly in urban and suburban watersheds. Impairment is caused by stormwater runoff increasing stream volume and flow rates that result in hydromodification in streams, stream erosion and sedimentation, flooding, and overflows in combined sewer systems. In addition, stormwater diminishes water quality by adding pollutants (e.g., solids, nutrients, metals) as it runs over land and into streams.

To successfully reduce pollution, municipalities must adopt innovative technologies at all stages of implementation, operation, and maintenance. One approach utilizes stormwater green infrastructure (GI), such as bioretention, constructed wetlands, vegetated roofs, and cisterns. Green infrastructure reduces watershed runoff volume, decreases runoff discharge rates through the watershed, and promotes infiltration and evapotranspiration to better mimic an undeveloped hydrologic cycle and filter water quality pollutants. Green infrastructure is becoming the preferred method of stormwater management, not only for small parcels of land but entire municipalities. A number of cities across the U.S. (e.g., New York, Washington D.C., Omaha, Austin, Cincinnati, and Seattle) are turning to GI to mitigate stormwater pollution issues. Despite the promise of GI systems to effectively address stormwater issues, adoption has been slow due to several factors, which can be classified generally as technological and human.

This project is an academic-industry research partnerships to address the central issue of identifying and advancing key platform technologies so as to enable “smart” service systems to enter into the commercialization process and to help insure positive social outcomes that would result from successful commercialization”, the proposal has the following goals:

  • Develop new monitoring and control algorithms integrated into a platform technology, OptiRTC, that automatically monitors and controls stormwater GI systems to:
    • Optimize performance of GI systems for water quantity and quality
    • Self-correct and maximize efficiency of GI systems
    • Minimize operation and maintenance costs
    • Integrate policy directly into design and operation
  • Accelerate the implementation of state-of-the-art smart stormwater services to increase the potential applications and market value of OptiRTC and meet national, regional, and local policy and financing needs in stormwater management
  • Enable synergistic activities to grow and develop academic and research faculty and the industry partner through interdisciplinary and cross-organizational collaboration and prepare graduate research students to become the next generation of professionals with an understanding of technology enterprise
  • Incite innovation in secondary markets
  • Encourage GI implementation across scales as the benefits become clearer
